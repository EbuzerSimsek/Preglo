Kick counting

How to count baby kicks

Practical guidance for personal pregnancy tracking — written to be clear, calm, and honest about limits.

What kick counting actually is

Kick counting is a simple habit: you pay attention to fetal movements — rolls, jabs, flutters, whooshes — during a quiet window so you learn what is normal for your baby. It is not a score to impress anyone. It is pattern awareness.

Babies have sleep stretches and active stretches. The goal is noticing a clear change from recent days, not matching a stranger’s timing on the internet.

When people usually start

Many clinicians talk more about intentional movement awareness in the third trimester, often from around week 28, though guidance varies with your history. Follow your midwife or doctor if they ask you to start earlier or use a specific protocol.

Before that, mid-pregnancy flutters are still worth noticing casually — see weeks in our week-by-week guide — but formal counting is often a later habit.

A simple method you can repeat

  1. Pick a consistent time when baby is usually active (often evening).
  2. Get comfortable — side-lying or semi-reclined in a quiet place helps many people.
  3. Start a session in Preglo’s kick counter and tap for each distinct movement.
  4. Stop at your clinician’s target (commonly around 10 movements) or at the timed window they prefer.
  5. Save the session so you can compare today’s rhythm with yesterday’s without relying on memory.

What usually counts

Rolls, punches, and clear flutters typically count as movements. Continuous hiccup rhythms may be treated differently depending on your clinician’s advice — ask if you are unsure. Do not chase perfection; chase consistency.

When to get help

If movements feel much weaker or fewer than your baby’s usual pattern, contact your care provider or maternity triage the same day. Do not wait for an app metric to “confirm” worry. Read more in reduced fetal movement and the 10-kick count method.

FAQ

When should I count kicks?

Often in the third trimester at a consistent quiet time — follow your clinician’s advice for timing and targets.

What counts as a kick?

Rolls, jabs, and clear flutters usually count; ask your provider how to treat hiccups in your protocol.

Do I need exactly 10 kicks?

Some protocols use count-to-10; others use timed windows. Use the method your care team recommends.
